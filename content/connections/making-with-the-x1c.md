# Making with the Bambu X1C

One printer, four very different jobs. These notes share a toolchain (a Bambu Lab X1C, a
slicer, a finishing craft) but pull in genuinely opposite directions, which is what makes
them fun to read side by side.

## The thread

- **[Metal-look board game pieces (electroplating)](../topics/making/plated-game-components)**
  — 3D print plus a plating bath to grow real metal on plastic, at batch scale.
- **[Weighty board game pieces (cold-casting)](../topics/making/cold-cast-game-pieces)**
  — the heavier route: metal-powder resin cast from a printed master, with steel or
  tungsten fill for real heft.
- **[Plastic and tissue: 3D-printed model aircraft](../topics/making/3d-printed-tissue-aircraft)**
  — the inversion: here every gram is the enemy, so the same printer is pushed to make
  the *lightest* possible structure.
- **[White's tree frogs: a bioactive build](../topics/pets/whites-tree-frog-bioactive-japan)**
  — the sleeper member: the terrarium uses printed parts too, and makes the same
  materials call the aircraft note does (PETG, not PLA, where heat and humidity bite).

## Why it connects

The heft-versus-weight inversion is the heart of it: the game-piece notes want *mass*
(metal fill, casting, plating) while the aircraft note wants the *opposite* (foaming
LW-PLA, single-wall skins) and even has to fight the X1C's aggressive cooling to get
there. Same machine, opposite goals. Running underneath all four is a recurring
materials decision, **PLA versus PETG for durability**, and a shared ethics line:
personal use is fine, selling reproductions of trademarked designs is where the trouble
starts. Browse the `maker-toolchain` tag for the whole set.
