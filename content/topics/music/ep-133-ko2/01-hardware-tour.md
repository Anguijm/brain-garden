# Chapter 1 — Hardware tour

This is the "what does every button do" chapter. The K.O. II has a deliberately
small set of physical controls, and almost everything is reached by holding one
button and pressing another (a "combo"). Learn the controls here; the combos show
up throughout the course and are collected in
[chapter 17](17-troubleshooting-reference.md).

First, three things that surprise people coming from other gear (all FACT):
there is **no jog wheel / data dial**, there is **no row of 16 step buttons**, and
there is only **one fader**, not several. You navigate and change values with the
`-` / `+` buttons, two knobs, and that single assignable fader.

## The pads (the heart of it)

FACT: There are **12 pads**, and they are both **velocity sensitive** (how hard you
hit changes the volume) and **pressure sensitive** (how hard you hold can sweep an
effect or modulate the sound). The pads do triple duty:

- they trigger the 12 sounds of the current group,
- they act as a **number pad** (1-9, 0) for typing values, slot numbers, and
  system codes,
- they become **effect controls** while you hold `FX`.

## The two knobs and the one fader

FACT: There are exactly **two knobs**, labelled `knob X` (the orange "A") and
`knob Y` (the black "B"), plus **one grey `FADER`**. Some reviews say "three knobs",
they are counting the fader. These three controls do the bulk of the tweaking, and
what they do changes per mode:

- `knob X` / `knob Y` adjust the two parameters of whatever you are editing (for
  example cutoff and resonance on the filter, start and end on a chop). Hold
  `SHIFT` while turning for fine adjustment.
- The `FADER` is **assignable per group**. By default it sets the group's level;
  hold the `FADER` button and pick a destination printed above the pads to point it
  at pitch, pan, filter, attack/release, time-stretch, or FX amount instead. Its
  movement can be recorded as automation (see [chapter 9](09-fader-and-automation.md)).

## The transport and mode buttons

FACT, names taken from the official guide:

- `PLAY` — start and stop playback.
- `RECORD` (the circle) — arm and record. On its own then `PLAY` gives a four-beat
  count-in; held, it lets you place hits step by step.
- `MAIN` — the home / mixer view, and the "back out of here" button.
- `SOUND` — enter Sound mode to pick and assign sounds to pads.
- `SAMPLE` — enter sampling mode to record audio.
- `TEMPO` — set tempo; tap it repeatedly for tap tempo.
- `TIMING` — set the note interval (the grid) and trigger note repeat.
- `FX` — hold to play the punch-in effects on the pads.
- `ERASE` — hold with a target (a pad, a group, the fader) to delete it.
- `SHIFT` — the "gateway" modifier; held with almost any button it reaches a second
  function. (The secondary functions are printed on the panel; reach them with
  `SHIFT`, and reach the fader's parameter row by holding the `FADER` button.)
- `GROUP A`, `GROUP B`, `GROUP C`, `GROUP D` — select which of the four groups you
  are working on.
- `ENTER` — confirm a selection or typed value (also reached as a pad in menus).
- `-` / `+` — the universal "previous / next" and value nudge: step through
  patterns, scenes, sounds, bars, effects, and steps, and toggle record feel.

Assessment: the mental shortcut is "press a mode button to go somewhere, hold
`SHIFT` to reach its deeper layer, hold `ERASE` to remove things, and use `-`/`+`
plus the two knobs to change values." Once that clicks, the panel stops feeling
cryptic.

## The screen

FACT: The display is a "super segment hybrid" screen: a small three-character
readout (think digital watch) surrounded by a fixed bank of 66 backlit icons. It
is not a pixel screen, so it shows short codes and lit icons rather than waveforms
or menus. You will see short status flashes such as `TRK` (track), `PTN`
(pattern), `SND` (sound), `BAR`, `DEL`/`CLR` (deleted/cleared), and `FUL` (memory
full). Learning to read these little codes is most of the learning curve.

## Ports and power

FACT: Along the top edge there are **six 3.5 mm mini-jacks**: main stereo
**output** (headphone/line), stereo **input** (for sampling), **sync in**, **sync
out**, **MIDI in**, and **MIDI out**. The MIDI jacks are TRS Type A, so you need a
TRS-to-5-pin-DIN adapter to talk to classic MIDI gear. (Verify the exact
left-to-right order on your unit; the existence of all six is certain, the order is
not printed in the spec.)

FACT: There is also a **USB-C** port (power, MIDI, firmware updates, and sample
transfer), a **built-in microphone**, and a **built-in speaker**. Power comes from
USB-C or **four AAA batteries** (about 20 hours on alkalines); it switches to USB
power automatically when plugged in.
