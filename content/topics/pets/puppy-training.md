# Puppy training: a practical starter guide

This is the short, usable version: what to do in the first few months, why it
works, and the handful of mistakes that cause most of the trouble. The science
is at the bottom if you want it, but you don't need it to get started.

## The one idea everything rests on

Reward the behavior you want; don't punish the behavior you don't.

FACT: Reward-based training (giving the puppy something it wants, like a treat,
to encourage a behavior) is what veterinary and kennel-club sources recommend as
the foundation. They specifically advise *against* punishment-based methods
(leash corrections, yelling, choke/shock/pinch collars), because punishment tends
to confuse the dog, create fear, and can damage its trust in you (OSU Veterinary
Medical Center; American Kennel Club).

The practical rule that follows: when your puppy does something wrong, your job is
usually to *prevent and redirect*, not to scold. Set the puppy up to succeed,
then reward the success.

## The first window matters most (3 weeks to ~3–4 months)

FACT: There is a "critical socialization period" running from roughly 3 weeks to
about 12 weeks of age (some sources extend it to 16 weeks depending on breed).
During this window puppies most readily form lasting comfort with new people,
animals, places, and experiences (OSU VMC; PMC socialisation study).

FACT: Poor socialization in this window is linked to later fearfulness, anxiety,
noise phobias, and fear-based aggression toward people or other dogs (OSU VMC).

What to do about it:
- Expose the puppy, gently and positively, to lots of new people, sounds,
  surfaces, and situations. Don't overwhelm it; you want curiosity, not panic.
- Assessment: A well-run puppy socialization class (safe, controlled, vaccinated
  group) is one of the easiest ways to do this, and one study cited by OSU found
  puppies who attended were more likely to stay in their homes long term.
- Assessment: Veterinary behaviorist Dr. Ian Dunbar's well-known rule of thumb is
  "100 people before 16 weeks." Treat it as a memorable target, not a precise
  prescription, the underlying study isn't cited directly.

The point is quantity *and* good quality of early experiences. This window closes,
and it's much harder to make up the ground later.

## Housetraining (potty training)

FACT: A common rule of thumb is that a puppy can "hold it" for about one hour per
month of age, up to roughly 10 hours maximum (OSU VMC). A 2-month-old puppy needs
a bathroom break at least every couple of hours.

The method that works (OSU VMC):
1. **Supervise constantly** when the puppy is loose. If you can't watch it, it
   goes in its crate or pen. Most accidents happen the moment you look away.
2. **Take it out roughly every hour**, to the same spot, with a consistent cue
   word ("potty"). Go *with* it so you can reward immediately.
3. **Reward the instant it finishes outside**, treat plus happy praise. You're
   building "going outside = good things happen."
4. **Confine when you're away.** A non-housetrained puppy should never be loose
   in the house alone.

FACT: Do not punish a puppy after the fact for an indoor accident. It does not
connect the punishment to the act; the "guilty look" is just fear of your anger.
Punishing after the fact often teaches the puppy to hide where it goes (behind
the couch, in another room), which makes training harder, not easier (OSU VMC).

## Crate training

FACT: The crate should be just big enough for the puppy to stand, turn around,
and lie down. Too much space lets it sleep in one corner and eliminate in
another, which defeats the purpose (OSU VMC). For a puppy that'll grow large, get
the big crate but block off the extra space for now.

Make it a good place, not a punishment (OSU VMC):
- Put it where the family spends time. Toss treats and toys inside; let the puppy
  wander in and out with the door open.
- Feed meals in the crate, then start closing the door while it eats.
- Build up alone-time gradually with a chew toy, leaving the room for short
  stretches first.
- Don't let the puppy out *while* it's crying, or you teach it that crying works.
  Wait for a quiet moment, then open the door. If crying is severe, you're likely
  moving too fast, slow down. Never use the crate as punishment.

## Teaching the basics

FACT: Puppies can start simple training as soon as they come home, around 8 weeks
old. Keep sessions short, about 5 to 10 minutes, and always end on a success so
the puppy stays happy to train (AKC).

FACT: Use something the puppy genuinely values as the reward, usually a small,
high-value food treat (AKC). Praise, play, and petting can become rewards too,
but praise works best once the puppy has learned to associate it with treats.

The AKC's "Basic 5" cues, each taught by rewarding the puppy the moment it does
the right thing (full step-by-step methods are in the AKC source):

- **Come (recall):** Start indoors and quiet. Say the name/"come" and treat,
  building up to the puppy turning and running to you. Make coming to you *fun*;
  never call the puppy to you and then do something it dislikes. Don't repeat the
  name over and over when ignored, that just teaches it to tune you out.
- **Loose-leash walking:** Goal is walking without pulling, not a strict show-ring
  heel. Reward the puppy for staying near your knee; turn the other way when it
  forges ahead. Pick a side and be consistent.
- **Sit:** Either "capture" it (reward whenever it sits on its own) or "lure" it
  (raise a treat over its nose so its bottom drops). Never push it into position.
- **Down:** Same idea as sit; lure the treat from the nose down to the floor.
- **Stay:** Teach a "release word" first ("OK"/"free"), then reward staying for
  gradually longer, then gradually add distance. Keep it easy enough to succeed.

## Mouthing and biting

FACT: Puppy mouthing is normal exploration, not aggression, and part of your job
is teaching "bite inhibition" (OSU VMC). Tips from OSU VMC:
- Avoid rough play involving the puppy's mouth and your hands.
- Redirect to chew toys (a smear of peanut butter makes them appealing).
- A high-pitched "ouch" startles many puppies into stopping, then redirect. (If
  your puppy treats the yelp as exciting attention, drop this one.)
- Remove attention when it gets mouthy: no eye contact, get up, walk away. The
  puppy learns biting ends the fun.

## Don't quit at adolescence

FACT: Dog adolescence typically starts around six months and runs to 18 months or
later depending on breed, and it's the period owners find most frustrating, the
puppy gets more independent, more energetic, and "forgets" its training (OSU VMC).

Assessment: This is the stage where a lot of owners give up, but it's exactly when
consistency pays off. Keep socializing, keep rewarding good manners (especially
not jumping up), and burn off energy with exercise, a tired dog is a better-behaved
dog. Positive-reinforcement obedience classes after six months help.

## What the research adds (optional)

These are findings from peer-reviewed studies. They're interesting context rather
than things you need for day-to-day training, and they're labeled as assessments
because they come from individual studies, not settled consensus.

- Assessment: A study using a "judgment bias" test found dogs trained with two or
  more aversive (punishment-based) methods were more "pessimistic," slower to
  approach ambiguous situations, suggesting a more negative mood state. The
  authors note they can't fully prove the direction of cause and effect (Nature
  Scientific Reports, 2021).
- Assessment: In a controlled comparison, dogs generally preferred food over toys,
  and food worked as a more effective training reinforcer, which supports the
  common advice to train with treats (PMC, 2023).
- Assessment: Trainability appears partly heritable. A study of Belgian Malinois
  linked variants near the *TAC1* gene to excitability and trainability, but the
  authors stress this is breed-specific and needs replication (PMC, 2024).
- Speculation: Brain-imaging (fMRI) research on working detector dogs has found
  neural patterns that predict training success before training begins. This could
  one day help select service dogs (which cost ~$20,000–$50,000 to train), but
  it's a research frontier with no relevance to training a pet puppy today
  (PMC, 2024).

## Gaps worth knowing

- The "100 people by 16 weeks" figure and several potty/crate rules of thumb are
  widely repeated but not always traced to a primary study. They're sensible
  starting points, not laws.
- This guide covers the foundations. It does not go deep on specific problem
  behaviors like resource guarding, separation anxiety, or serious leash
  reactivity, those are worth a vet behaviorist's help if they show up.
