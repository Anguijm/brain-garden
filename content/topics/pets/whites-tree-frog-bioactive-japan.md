# White's tree frogs: care, and a bioactive build for Japan

Two notes in one. The first half is the care basics for White's tree frogs
(*Litoria caerulea*, "dumpy tree frog"), drawn from several independent care
sources that agree closely. The second half is the specific bioactive build for a
45×45×60 cm Exo Terra in Kanagawa, with the exact hardware, plus an honest pass
over where the planning conversation with Gemini needs a second look.

A labeling note up front: the husbandry numbers below come from multiple hobbyist
and care-sheet sources that converge, not from a primary veterinary text (the vet
care sheet I tried to pull wouldn't load). So most specifics are marked
**Assessment (care-sheet consensus)** rather than FACT. They're a sound starting
point; if a number really matters, confirm it with an exotics vet.

---

## Part 1 — Care basics

### The frog

Assessment (care-sheet consensus): White's tree frogs are hardy, long-lived,
arboreal (tree-dwelling) and nocturnal. Adults reach about 3–5 inches (8–13 cm)
snout-to-vent, females larger than males, and they commonly live **15–20+ years**
in captivity, so this is a long commitment.

FACT: They are insectivores (they eat live invertebrates), and across every
source the single most common health problem in captivity is **obesity** from
overfeeding. (The species is also famously prone to it because they'll eat
whatever you offer.)

Note on the name: you'll see both *Litoria caerulea* and *Ranoidea caerulea* in
sources. That's a real, recent taxonomic reclassification of the same animal, not
two species.

### Enclosure

Assessment (care-sheet consensus): The widely cited **minimum for a single frog
is 18″×18″×24″ (45×45×60 cm)** — exactly the Exo Terra 4560 in this build.
Height matters because they climb. For groups of 2–4, care sheets recommend
*going bigger* (e.g. 24″×18″×36″ or 36″×18″×36″). See the flag in Part 3: this
build puts **two** frogs in the single-frog minimum.

### Temperature

Assessment (care-sheet consensus): A vertical gradient, warm at the top, cool at
the bottom:
- **Basking / top zone:** ~28–29°C (82–84°F)
- **Ambient / mid:** ~23–25°C (74–76°F)
- **Night:** can safely fall to ~18–20°C (down to ~65°F)

This matches the targets from the build planning (28°C basking, ~22–24°C floor,
18–20°C night).

### Humidity

Assessment (care-sheet consensus): Aim for **~50% ambient with daily spikes to
~70%** after misting. Critically, *don't* hold it at 80%+ around the clock,
sustained high humidity with poor airflow causes respiratory and skin problems.
Let the tank dry down somewhat between mistings. A mesh-top Exo Terra naturally
helps with that airflow.

### Lighting and UVB

Assessment (care-sheet consensus): UVB is **beneficial but not strictly required**
for survival; it helps vitamin-D3 synthesis, calcium use, and general health. A
low-output bulb is right (e.g. a 5.0 / "UVB 100" or an Arcadia ShadeDweller),
positioned so the frog's back can't get closer than ~6″ (15 cm) to the lamp.
Replace UVB bulbs every ~12 months (output fades before the light dies). Run all
lights on a **12-hour day/night cycle**. A separate plant-grow LED is needed for
live plants in a bioactive tank.

### Diet and supplements

Assessment (care-sheet consensus):
- **Staples:** crickets, dubia/discoid roaches, black soldier fly larvae,
  silkworms, earthworms.
- **Occasional only:** hornworms, superworms (fatty). Avoid mealworms as a staple
  (hard chitin); waxworms are too fatty; a pinky mouse is at most a rare treat.
- **Frequency:** adults every **2–4 days**, juveniles daily to every other day.
  Err toward less, given the obesity risk. A useful obesity tell: the bony ridge
  above the eyes disappearing under fat folds.
- **Supplements:** dust feeders with calcium + D3 and a multivitamin; you can dust
  less often if you run UVB.

### Water

Assessment (care-sheet consensus): Use **dechlorinated tap water or spring
water** for the water dish and misting. Notably, at least one care source
explicitly says **avoid distilled / reverse-osmosis water** for the frogs. This
directly contradicts part of the build plan, see the flag in Part 3.

### Handling and cohabitation

Assessment (care-sheet consensus): They *tolerate* gentle handling better than
most frogs but don't enjoy it; keep it brief (5–10 min), and always wet your
washed hands first so you don't strip their protective slime coat or transfer
soap/lotion. House only with same-size conspecifics (size-match to avoid
feeding-time intimidation); never mix amphibian species (disease/stress).

## Part 2 — The bioactive build (Exo Terra 4560, Kanagawa)

This is the planned setup. It's a sound bioactive design; the hardware is chosen
for what's actually available in Japan.

### Layers (bottom to top)
- **Drainage:** ~2″ of LECA (clay pebbles) so the soil never sits in water.
- **Barrier:** fine mesh (e.g. Exo Terra BioDrain) so soil doesn't fall into the
  pebbles.
- **Substrate:** a chunky, water-retentive mix. Working recipe from planning:
  ~60% coco-coir/peat, 20% orchid bark, 10% worm castings, 10% horticultural
  charcoal. (A commercial ABG-style mix also works.)
- **Leaf litter:** a thick blanket of oak/magnolia leaves. This keeps the frogs'
  sticky skin out of the dirt and houses the cleanup crew. **Don't collect leaves
  from outside** (pesticides, parasites); buy dried beetle-breeding leaves
  (クヌギの落ち葉) and flash-boil 5–10 min to sterilize before use.

### Custom foam background
Black pond/landscape expanding foam (発泡ウレタン, the dark "pond use" 池用 type),
carved back to the porous texture, then coated with **100% silicone + dry
coco-coir**. The silicone must be **non-antifungal** (防カビ剤なし) — mold-inhibitor
silicone is toxic to amphibians. Allow **3–5 days off-gassing** before any
animals or cleanup crew go in. 3D-printed planter pockets/ledges embed into the
wet foam; print them in **PETG, not PLA** (PLA degrades in a warm, humid,
bioactive tank).

### Cleanup crew (add ~1–2 weeks before the frogs)
- **Isopods:** Powder Orange / Powder Blue (*Porcellionides pruinosus*) — fast,
  hardy, breed quickly, ~20–30 to start. Optionally dwarf whites
  (*Trichorhina tomentosa*) as an underground secondary crew.
- **Springtails:** one temperate-white culture, dumped onto the damp soil.
- Seed them first and let them establish for 10–14 days (feed a pinch of fish
  flakes / a zucchini slice) so a hidden generation exists before "frog pressure"
  begins. Japanese search terms: springtails トビムシ, isopods ワラジムシ.

### Plants (frog-proof)
Heavy frogs crush flimsy leaves. Stick to sturdy species: pothos, snake plant
(*Dracaena*), ZZ plant, bird's nest fern, plus mosses tucked into the background.

### Hardware (Japan-available)
- **Heat fixture:** GEX Exo Terra Light Dome 14cm (PT2055), aluminum, ceramic
  socket, sits on the mesh.
- **Heat bulb:** the plan landed on a **GEX Exo Terra Ceramic Heater 50W**
  (JAN 4972547039477) after the preferred Arcadia Deep Heat Projector turned out
  to be unavailable in Japan. (See Part 3 for the trade-off and a better-fit
  alternative.)
- **Thermostat:** REPTITRIP PT02 — a PID *dimming* thermostat. Heat bulb plugs
  into this; it holds a steady temperature rather than clicking fully on/off.
- **Probe placement:** secured high in the basking zone, ~10–15 cm below the mesh,
  where the frogs can't sit on it (a frog on the probe fools the thermostat into
  overheating the tank) and out of the direct misting spray.
- **Light canopy:** Exo Terra Compact Top with a UVB 100 (13W) coil + a 6500K
  plant LED. (The first plant bulb was defective, see Part 3.) Run the canopy on a
  plain wall timer, **not** through the dimming thermostat.
- **Mister:** GEX Exo Terra Monsoon Solo (PT2494). Starting cycle: every **8
  hours for ~12 seconds**, then tune to hold 50–70% by your hygrometer. Use
  **distilled/purified water in the mister to protect the nozzles** from mineral
  clogging (this is fine for the *machine*; the frogs' drinking dish is a separate
  question, see Part 3).
- **Monitoring:** a SwitchBot or ThermoPro thermometer/hygrometer with phone
  alerts.

## Part 3 — Where the Gemini plan needs a second look

The build conversation was with an AI (Gemini), which is helpful but unverified.
These are the points where it conflicts with the care-sheet consensus or oversells
a recommendation. None are disasters; they're decisions worth making with eyes
open.

1. **Two frogs in a single-frog minimum tank.** Care sheets call 18×18×24 the
   minimum for *one* frog and say go bigger for 2–4. Gemini treated the 4560 as
   fine for two. It's commonly done and these frogs are sedentary, so it's
   workable, but it's the floor, not roomy. Watch for feeding competition and
   keep the two size-matched. If you ever add a third, you'll want a bigger tank.

2. **Water type contradiction.** Gemini said use distilled/purified water; a care
   source says explicitly *avoid* distilled/RO water for the frogs. Both can be
   right about different things: distilled is good **for the mister** (won't clog
   nozzles or spot the glass), but the frogs' **soaking/drinking dish should be
   dechlorinated tap or spring water**, not pure distilled, amphibians absorb
   water and some electrolytes through their skin. Easiest path: distilled in the
   mister, dechlorinated tap (or spring water) in the water dish. Worth a quick
   confirm with a local exotics vet.

3. **Ceramic heater vs. a basking bulb.** The plan fell back to a CHE only because
   the Arcadia Deep Heat Projector isn't sold in Japan. Two caveats: (a) at least
   one care source actually *discourages* ceramic emitters and prefers a
   low-wattage **white basking bulb** (which gives a light+heat basking spot a
   day-active basking frog can use); (b) a CHE dries the air faster, so you'll
   lean harder on the mister to hold humidity. The CHE is fine on the PT02, but a
   white basking spot bulb over the basking branch (on the dimming thermostat) is
   worth considering as the daytime heat source, with the CHE reserved for night
   if needed.

4. **DHP availability and the "best DHP fixture" advice.** Gemini spent a lot of
   the chat optimizing around the Arcadia DHP, then discovered it's not
   distributed in Japan. If you specifically want the deep-heat (infrared-A/B)
   benefit, importing from Amazon US/UK is the only reliable route (voltage
   adapter + 1–2 week wait). Otherwise the CHE/basking-bulb path above is the
   pragmatic local choice, don't over-invest in DHP-specific accessories unless
   you're committed to importing the bulb.

5. **The flickering plant LED.** The Exo Terra Forest Canopy LED that flickered
   after warming up is a **failing/defective bulb** (heat-triggered driver
   fault), not a 50Hz-grid problem (a true frequency mismatch shows immediately).
   Returning it was the right call. A plain Japanese 6500K household LED
   (昼光色, Panasonic/Toshiba) or a GEX Clear LED bar is a stable replacement.

6. **General reminder on AI specifics.** Gemini gave very confident model numbers,
   JAN codes, shop addresses, and "best fit" picks from photos. Treat those as
   leads to verify in person, not gospel, especially store inventory (Hachikura
   in Yokosuka, Kanedai, Cainz, Joyful Honda, Wild Monster and Charm online were
   all suggested, but stock fluctuates).

## Quick build order

1. Build the foam background; cure and off-gas 3–5 days.
2. Layer drainage → mesh → substrate → boiled leaf litter.
3. Plant it; install lights, heat, thermostat probe, mister.
4. Run it empty a few days; dial temp (28°C basking) and humidity (50–70%).
5. Seed the cleanup crew; let it establish 10–14 days.
6. Add the two frogs. Feed lightly; watch weight.
