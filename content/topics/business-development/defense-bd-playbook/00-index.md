# Defense BD Playbook

This is a working playbook for winning U.S. defense and federal business. It pulls
together the standard practitioner frameworks (the Shipley capture lifecycle, the
APMP proposal body of knowledge, common gate-review and price-to-win practice) and
grounds the regulatory pieces in the actual rules (the FAR, DFARS, GAO bid-protest
forum, SAM.gov). The tradecraft is labeled **Assessment** because it is industry
convention rather than law; the regulatory specifics are labeled **FACT** with a
citation. Where a number changes over time (dollar thresholds, FAR versions),
verify the current value before you rely on it.

A note on how to read this. Business development in defense is not sales. Selling
is what happens in the last 90 days around a released solicitation. BD is the
12-to-36-month game of choosing the right pursuits, getting known by the customer,
and shaping the requirement so that when the solicitation drops you are already
the most credible answer. If you only show up when the Request for Proposal (RFP)
is public, you have usually already lost. Everything here serves that earlier game.

## The one idea everything rests on

Assessment: You win defense work before the RFP is released, by lowering the
customer's perceived risk of choosing you. Price matters, past performance
matters, technical approach matters, but underneath all of it the source-selection
authority is asking one question: "who is least likely to fail me?" Your whole BD
and capture effort is a campaign to make the answer obviously you, and to have
shaped the evaluation so that what you do best is what gets rewarded.

## How to use it

If you are standing up a BD function, read it in order. If you are running a live
pursuit, jump to chapter 1 for the lifecycle, chapter 2 to make the bid/no-bid
call, then chapters 4 and 6 to build the capture plan. The templates in chapter 11
are meant to be copied and filled in per opportunity.
