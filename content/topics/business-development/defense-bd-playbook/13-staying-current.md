# Chapter 13 — Staying current

This field moves: thresholds adjust, the NDAA rewrites authorities every year, LPTA
rules tighten, new vehicles and OT consortia appear, and protest case law shifts
what evaluators can do. This chapter is a standing research kit so the playbook
stays a living document. Assessment: pick a cadence (a 30-minute weekly skim plus a
deeper monthly pass works well), run the searches, and when something material
changes, update the relevant chapter.

How to feed it back into the vault: any of the search terms below is a ready-made
topic. Text it to the brain research Signal group, or run the expand engine
(`_scripts/expand.py "<term>"`), and the change gets staged, verified, and folded
into the right chapter. The single most useful recurring query is the FAR/DFARS
threshold and acquisition-reform check, because those are the facts most likely to
go stale (see [chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md)).

## Search terms by theme

Copy these into a web search, a news alert, or the vault engine. Add the current
year (for example "2026") to freshen any of them.

**Policy, regulation, and reform**
- `FAR DFARS acquisition-related threshold inflation adjustment current`
- `Revolutionary FAR Overhaul status` / `FAR rewrite deviation guidance`
- `NDAA acquisition provisions summary` (yearly; this is where authorities change)
- `DoD LPTA restrictions best value tradeoff policy update`
- `DoD source selection procedures guide update`
- `commercial solutions opening CSO guidance DoD`
- `CMMC cybersecurity DFARS 252.204-7012 7021 requirements update`

**Budget and opportunity intelligence**
- `DoD President's Budget RDT&E procurement justification books` (the "J-books")
- `[agency/PEO name] long range acquisition forecast`
- `[program name] acquisition strategy industry day sources sought`
- `unfunded priorities list [service] fiscal year`

**Contract vehicles, OT, and nontraditional**
- `new GWAC IDIQ on-ramp [domain, e.g. IT, cyber, space]`
- `Other Transaction consortium [mission area] how to join`
- `OTA prototype to production 10 USC 4022(f) examples`
- `SBIR STTR Phase III sole source [agency] topics`
- `mentor-protege program DoD SBA changes`

**Capture and proposal tradecraft**
- `Shipley capture management process update`
- `APMP body of knowledge proposal best practices`
- `price-to-win analysis methodology defense`
- `proposal color team review best practices`
- `government oral presentations proposal trends`
- `generative AI proposal management govcon policy` / `AI overconfidence
  decision-making research` (see [chapter 10](10-gen-ai-in-bd.md))

**Market and competitive intelligence**
- `[competitor name] contract awards USASpending` / `defense award announcements`
- `defense M&A consolidation [your segment]`
- `[incumbent] recompete [program] award protest`
- `GovWin / Bloomberg Government [opportunity or agency] report`

**Protests and case law**
- `GAO bid protest annual report to Congress statistics` (yearly; sustain rates and
  most common grounds)
- `recent GAO sustained protest [domain]` / `COFC bid protest decision`
- `organizational conflict of interest OCI protest decision recent`

**Mission and technology trends (for shaping, see [chapter 4](04-customer-stakeholders-shaping.md))**
- `[your mission area, e.g. C2, ISR, EW, autonomy, space, cyber] DoD priorities`
- `replicator initiative / CDAO / software acquisition pathway update`
- `[customer]'s stated modernization priorities posture statement`

## Sources worth monitoring

Assessment: a small set of reliable sources beats a sprawling feed. Group them by
how authoritative they are.

**Primary / government (the ground truth)**
- acquisition.gov — the FAR and DFARS themselves, and the Federal Acquisition
  Circulars (FAC) that announce changes.
- SAM.gov — opportunity notices, sources-sought, awards (your live pipeline feed).
- GAO.gov — the bid-protest forum and the annual bid-protest report to Congress.
- DAU.edu (Defense Acquisition University) — the Adaptive Acquisition Framework and
  acquisition policy explainers.
- USASpending.gov — who won what, for competitive and budget intelligence.
- SBIR.gov and the SBA — innovation programs and small-business rules.
- The annual NDAA and the service posture statements / budget justification books.

**Trade press and analysis**
- Washington Technology, Federal News Network, Defense News, Breaking Defense,
  Inside Defense, Federal Computer Week (FCW) / Nextgov.
- Law-firm govcon blogs for protest and OCI analysis (for example
  SmallGovCon / Koprince, Crowell, Wiley, PilieroMazza). Useful, vendor-adjacent,
  read as informed commentary not law.

**Professional bodies and tradecraft**
- APMP (Association of Proposal Management Professionals) and Shipley Associates for
  capture and proposal practice.
- NCMA (National Contract Management Association) for the contracting side.
- PSC (Professional Services Council) and NDIA for industry-policy positioning.

**Market intelligence platforms (paid)**
- GovWin IQ (Deltek), Bloomberg Government, and similar, for forecasts, pipeline,
  and competitor tracking. Assessment: high value if your pipeline justifies the
  cost.

## Setting up alerts

Assessment: turn the searches above into push, not pull, so the field comes to you.

- Create saved searches and interested-vendor alerts on SAM.gov for your target
  agencies, NAICS codes, and keywords.
- Set news alerts (your search engine's alert feature or your trade-press
  subscriptions) on the policy and competitor terms above.
- Subscribe to the FAC notifications on acquisition.gov so threshold and clause
  changes reach you directly.
- Calendar the yearly anchors: the NDAA (late each year), the President's Budget
  (early each year), and the GAO bid-protest annual report.

## Keeping the playbook honest

Assessment: when a search turns up a real change, do two things. Update the affected
chapter with the new fact and its citation, and relabel correctly (FACT only for
what you verified against the primary source, per the vault's rules). A playbook
that quietly goes stale is worse than no playbook, because people trust it. This
chapter is how you keep that from happening.
