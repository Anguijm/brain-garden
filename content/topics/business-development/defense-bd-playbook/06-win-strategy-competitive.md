# Chapter 6 — Win strategy and competitive analysis

Win strategy is the bridge between knowing the customer and writing the proposal.
It answers one question: why will they choose us over the specific people we are
fighting? Assessment: a win strategy that is not built against named competitors
and real evaluation criteria is just a wish list.

## Discriminators, not strengths

A strength is something you are good at. A discriminator is something you do that
matters to this customer and that your competitors cannot credibly claim.
Assessment: customers do not reward strengths they can get from anyone. Force every
proposed strength through three tests: does the customer value it (is it tied to
their stated need or evaluation criteria), is it true and provable, and is it
ours alone? Only what passes all three is a real discriminator, and your win
strategy should be built on a small number of them.

## Win themes

A win theme is a repeated, evidence-backed message that connects a customer hot
button to your discriminator and to a benefit. The reliable shape is: customer
need, plus your discriminating feature, equals a proof point and a benefit to
them. Assessment: a handful of strong win themes, woven consistently through every
volume, beats a proposal that lists many undifferentiated strengths. Decide the
themes during capture, not during writing.

## Ghosting the competition

Ghosting is raising the customer's awareness of a competitor's weakness without
naming them, by stressing the value of something you do well and they do not.
Assessment: done with restraint it sharpens your discriminators; overdone it reads
as negative and can backfire. Never disparage a competitor by name. Instead, make
the evaluation criteria you are strong on feel essential, so the customer notices
the gap on their own.

## Black Hat review

A Black Hat is a structured session where your team role-plays each major
competitor and builds their likely bid: their probable solution, price posture,
discriminators, and how they will attack you. Assessment: the goal is to see your
pursuit through the enemy's eyes, find where you are genuinely exposed, and adjust
your strategy and shaping while there is still time. Run it during capture, well
before proposal start, and feed the findings into your gap-closure plan and your
own discriminator and price decisions.

## Competitive intelligence (the honest kind)

Build the competitor picture from legitimate sources: public award histories and
USASpending data, the incumbent's CPARS reputation as you can learn it, the
competitor's published capabilities and personnel moves, your debriefs on past
deals, and customer signals. Assessment: never use procurement-sensitive or
proprietary competitor information, the integrity rules in
[chapter 8](08-relationships-ethics-oci.md) are absolute, and a tainted bid is a
lost bid plus legal exposure.

## Price-to-win (PTW)

Price-to-win is your analytic estimate of the price at which you would most likely
win while still being able to perform and make acceptable margin. Assessment: PTW
is not "our cost plus a markup," it is a competitor- and customer-driven target
built from the likely budget, the incumbent's run rate, competitor cost
structures, and the evaluation method (a best-value tradeoff tolerates a price
premium for value; an LPTA buy does not, see
[chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md)).

Run PTW during capture so it can drive solution and teaming choices, then reconcile
it against your actual bottoms-up cost estimate. Where PTW is below your cost, you
have a problem to solve through scope, solution, team, or a decision to no-bid, not
a number to wish away. Forward pricing rate agreements and a competitive indirect
rate structure (FAR 15.407-3) are part of how you get your cost low enough to meet
PTW honestly.

## Putting it together

Assessment: the win strategy you carry into proposal planning should state, on one
page, the named competitors, your two to four real discriminators, the matching
win themes, the ghosting angles, the PTW target and your price posture, and the
specific gaps you are still closing. Everything in the proposal then serves that
page.
