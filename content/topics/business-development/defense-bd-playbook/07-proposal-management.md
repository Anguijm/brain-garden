# Chapter 7 — Proposal management

The proposal is where capture gets cashed in. Its job is narrow and unforgiving: be
compliant, be compelling, be clean, and be on time. Assessment: most losses at this
stage are self-inflicted, through non-compliance, missed instructions, or a
generic solution that ignored the win strategy. This chapter follows the
APMP-style discipline most defense shops use.

## Read the solicitation like a contract, because it is

A government solicitation has a standard structure (the Uniform Contract Format).
The four sections you live in:

- **Section C** (or the Statement of Work / Performance Work Statement): what you
  must do.
- **Section L:** instructions for preparing the proposal, format, page limits,
  volumes, submission method. Violate these and you can be thrown out.
- **Section M:** the evaluation factors and their relative importance, how you will
  be scored. Section M is the most important page in the document; you write to it.
- **Sections I, K, and the reps and certs:** clauses and certifications you must
  satisfy.

Assessment: read M first, then L, then C, then build everything to align them.

## The compliance matrix

Build a compliance matrix that decomposes every "shall," instruction, and
evaluation factor from Sections L, M, and C into a row, mapped to the proposal
section that answers it, with an owner. Assessment: this is non-negotiable and it
is the backbone of the proposal. Compliance is the price of admission; a brilliant
proposal that misses a required element can be rated non-compliant and eliminated
before its quality is even considered.

## Storyboards and the annotated outline

Before writers write prose, build an annotated outline (sometimes storyboards or
"mock-ups") for each section: the requirement it answers, the win themes and
discriminators to land there, the proof points, and the graphics. Assessment: this
forces the win strategy into the structure early and prevents the common failure of
compliant-but-generic text. Plan the graphics deliberately; evaluators skim, and a
strong action caption on a clear graphic often carries more than a page of prose.

## Color-team reviews

Color reviews are scheduled independent reviews at defined maturity stages.
Assessment: their value depends entirely on using fresh, senior reviewers with
clear instructions and protected time, and on the team actually acting on the
findings. The common sequence:

- **Pink Team:** early-draft review of approach and structure. Are we compliant,
  is the solution sound, are the win themes present? Best chance to course-correct.
- **Red Team:** near-final review scored as an evaluator would, against Section M.
  Is it compliant, compelling, and would it win? Findings drive the final rewrite.
- **Gold Team:** final executive review and approval to submit, including the
  price.
- **White-glove / production check:** the last pass for format, page limits,
  cross-references, and submission compliance before it goes out.

Some organizations add a **Blue Team** (early solution/strategy validation before
Pink) and a **Black Hat** (competitor simulation, during capture, see
[chapter 6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md)).

## The four Cs (a writing standard)

Assessment: hold every section to compliant (answers the requirement), compelling
(advances a discriminator and a benefit, not just a feature), clear (the evaluator
can find and score the answer fast), and correct (factually and contractually
accurate). Use the customer's language, lead with the point, and make the
evaluator's scoring job effortless.

## Pricing and the cost volume

The price proposal must be compliant, consistent with the technical volume (no
"disconnects" where the staffing in cost does not match the approach in
technical), and aligned to your price-to-win posture. FACT: for larger negotiated
actions, certified cost or pricing data may be required (FAR 15.403-4). Assessment:
build the cost volume in lockstep with the technical solution and review them
together, because evaluators specifically hunt for technical-cost mismatches.

## Submission

Follow Section L's submission mechanics exactly: format, file types, portal,
quantity, and above all the deadline. FACT: late proposals are generally rejected,
the rules on late submission are strict. Assessment: submit early, confirm receipt,
and never let an avoidable mechanical failure waste months of capture.

## After submission: ENs, discussions, orals, FPR, and the debrief

Be ready for evaluation notices (ENs), discussions, oral presentations, and a
request for a final proposal revision (FPR); prepare for orals with the same rigor
as the written volumes, because they are often decisive. FACT: you are entitled to
request a debrief, and pre-award and post-award debrief procedures are set in FAR
15.505 and 15.506. Always take the debrief, win or lose. Assessment: it is your
clearest read on how the customer actually evaluated you, the seed of your next
pursuit with them, and the basis for deciding whether a protest is warranted (see
[chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md)).
