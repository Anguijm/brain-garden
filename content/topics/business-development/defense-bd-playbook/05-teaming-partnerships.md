# Chapter 5 — Teaming and partnerships

Few defense pursuits are won alone. Teaming lets you cover capability gaps, add
relevant past performance, satisfy small-business requirements, and reduce the
customer's perceived risk by assembling visibly the right players. Assessment:
teaming decisions are strategic, make them during capture, not in a panic after
the RFP drops.

## Prime or sub?

Decide deliberately which role serves your strategy on each deal.

- **Prime contractor:** you hold the contract, own the customer relationship, and
  capture the margin and the past-performance credit, but you carry the risk and
  the management burden, and you must be credibly able to perform the core scope.
- **Subcontractor:** lower risk and a way to build relevant past performance on
  programs you cannot yet prime, but less control, thinner margin, and you depend
  on the prime's win. Assessment: subbing strategically on the right program can
  be the fastest route to the past performance you need to prime the next one.

A useful pattern: prime where you are strong and known, sub where you are building
a new customer or capability.

## Choosing teammates

Assessment: pick teammates for what the win actually requires, not for friendship.
Evaluate each candidate on capability fit, relevant and well-rated past
performance, customer standing, price posture, cultural and ethical reliability,
and any conflicts. A teammate with a poor CPARS record or an OCI problem can sink
your bid. Beware the teammate who is also quietly talking to your competitor.

## Small-business strategy

On set-aside work (see [chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md)), the small or
disadvantaged business must be the prime, and large businesses participate as
subs. On full-and-open work, your small-business subcontracting plan and goals are
themselves evaluated. Assessment: build genuine, repeatable relationships with
strong small businesses in your niches; mentor-protege arrangements can formalize
this and create durable advantage. Treat small-business partners as long-term
allies, not as checkboxes, because the community talks and reputation compounds.

## The paperwork that holds it together

- **Non-disclosure agreement (NDA):** sign first, before you exchange anything
  sensitive about the pursuit or your solution.
- **Teaming agreement (TA):** the pre-award document that sets out who is prime,
  the intended scope or workshare for each party, exclusivity, and the
  obligation to negotiate a subcontract in good faith if you win. Assessment: a TA
  is largely a statement of intent, courts have often found the actual workshare
  not fully enforceable until the subcontract is signed, so define workshare as
  concretely as you can and convert to a subcontract promptly after award.
- **Exclusivity:** decide whether teammates are exclusive to your team for this
  pursuit. Lock in the partners whose capability or past performance is essential.

## Workshare and the win

Assessment: allocate workshare to maximize probability of win first and your own
revenue second. Give a teammate the scope where they are genuinely strongest if
that strengthens the bid, because a higher PWin on a real win beats a larger share
of a loss. Make the team's combined story to the customer coherent: one solution,
clear roles, no seams.
