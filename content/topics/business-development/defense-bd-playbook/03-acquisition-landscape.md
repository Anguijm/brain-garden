# Chapter 3 — The acquisition landscape

You cannot run capture well without knowing the rules of the field. This chapter
is the compressed version of how the government is allowed to buy, because the
acquisition strategy the customer chooses determines your whole approach. The
regulatory facts here are cited; verify any dollar figure before relying on it,
because they are adjusted for inflation periodically.

## The rulebooks: FAR and DFARS

FACT: The Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) is the primary government-wide
rulebook for federal procurement (acquisition.gov/far). FACT: The Defense Federal
Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS) is the Department of Defense layer that
supplements and sometimes deviates from the FAR (acquisition.gov/dfars). When you
work a DoD deal, both apply, and DFARS clauses (for example cybersecurity and
business-system requirements) carry real compliance weight.

## Dollar thresholds that shape the path

FACT: As of October 1, 2025, the micro-purchase threshold is $15,000 and the
simplified acquisition threshold (SAT) is $350,000 (FAR 2.101, per the FAR
inflation-adjustment final rule, Federal Register, Aug 27 2025). These matter
because:

- Below the micro-purchase threshold, buying is very light-touch (often a purchase
  card).
- Below the SAT, simplified acquisition procedures (FAR Part 13) apply, and
  acquisitions are generally reserved for small business.
- Above the SAT, you are usually in full FAR Part 15 negotiated-procurement
  territory, with formal evaluation and the most capture-intensive competition.

## How awards get evaluated (FAR Part 15)

FACT: FAR Part 15 governs contracting by negotiation, the home of most large
competitive captures (acquisition.gov/far/part-15). Two evaluation approaches
matter most:

- **Best-value tradeoff (FAR 15.101-1):** the government may pay more for a
  higher-rated proposal, and may award to other than the lowest price or highest
  technical score, as long as the solicitation states the factors and their
  relative importance. This rewards discriminators and is where capture pays off.
- **Lowest-price technically acceptable, LPTA (FAR 15.101-2):** technical is
  pass/fail and the lowest acceptable price wins. Assessment: LPTA collapses your
  ability to win on quality, so on an LPTA buy the game is ruthless price and
  bulletproof compliance, and the bid/no-bid math is different. (Congress and DoD
  have restricted LPTA use in some contexts in recent years; check the current
  solicitation.)

FACT: FAR 15.201 expressly allows exchanges with industry before receipt of
proposals, which is the legal basis for the shaping activity in
[chapter 4](04-customer-stakeholders-shaping.md). FACT: pre-award and post-award
debriefs are provided for at FAR 15.505 and 15.506, your best post-decision
intelligence.

## Contract types (who carries the risk)

Assessment: the contract type sets the risk split and your margin profile.

- **Firm-fixed-price (FFP):** you bear cost risk, the customer likes certainty.
  Common for well-defined work.
- **Cost-reimbursement (cost-plus, e.g. CPFF/CPIF):** the government carries cost
  risk for less-defined or higher-risk work, but you live under cost-accounting
  and oversight requirements.
- **Time-and-materials (T&M) and labor-hour:** used when scope is hard to
  estimate; lighter on you but watched closely.

## Vehicles: how the work actually flows

Much defense work does not come as a standalone contract but as a task or delivery
order under a larger vehicle. Assessment: getting onto the right vehicles is a BD
objective in its own right, because you cannot bid task orders on a vehicle you do
not hold.

- **IDIQ (indefinite-delivery, indefinite-quantity):** an umbrella contract under
  which the agency competes task orders, often among a limited pool of awardees.
- **GWACs (government-wide acquisition contracts)** and **GSA Schedules:**
  pre-competed, government-wide vehicles for IT and services.
- **BPAs (blanket purchase agreements):** streamlined recurring-purchase
  arrangements, often off a Schedule.

Winning a seat on a multiple-award IDIQ or GWAC is a "hunting license"; the real
revenue fight is the task-order competitions that follow.

## Competition status and set-asides

Assessment: how the buy is competed changes who you fight.

- **Full and open competition:** anyone eligible may bid.
- **Small-business set-asides (FAR Part 19):** reserved for small businesses; sub
  categories include 8(a), HUBZone, Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned (SDVOSB), and
  Women-Owned (WOSB). The "rule of two" (FAR 19.502-2) drives many set-aside
  decisions: if two or more capable small businesses are expected to bid at fair
  prices, the buy is generally set aside.
- **Sole-source:** allowed in defined circumstances (including certain 8(a) and
  SBIR Phase III actions), and a major BD prize when you can justify it.

## OTAs and SBIR/STTR (the non-FAR and innovation lanes)

FACT: Other Transactions (OTs) are agreements outside the FAR, used by DoD to move
faster on research and prototypes. Research OTs are authorized under 10 U.S.C.
4021; prototype OTs under 10 U.S.C. 4022, and a prototype OT that was competitively
awarded and successfully completed can transition to a production OT without
re-competition under 10 U.S.C. 4022(f) (law.cornell.edu; DAU Adaptive Acquisition
Framework). Assessment: OTs, often run through consortia, are a fast on-ramp for
nontraditional contractors and a way to reach production without a fresh
competition, which makes them strategically valuable.

Assessment: the Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and STTR programs run in
phases: Phase I (feasibility, small award, months), Phase II (prototype/R&D,
larger award, roughly two years), and Phase III (commercialization, funded by
non-SBIR dollars). The key BD lever: Phase III work derived from an earlier SBIR
can often be awarded sole-source, which is a clean path from a small research win
to a larger production or services contract (sbir.gov).

## Bid protests (the backstop)

FACT: The Government Accountability Office (GAO) runs an independent bid-protest
forum and has adjudicated procurement disputes for over a century; protests can
challenge solicitation terms or award decisions (gao.gov/legal/bid-protests).
Assessment: protests are a real but double-edged tool. Use a debrief to decide
whether grounds exist, weigh the cost to the customer relationship, and remember
that an incumbent you displace may protest you too. Plan for protest risk in your
schedule.
