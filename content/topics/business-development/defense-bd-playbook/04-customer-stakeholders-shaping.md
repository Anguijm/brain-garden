# Chapter 4 — Customer, stakeholders, and shaping

In defense, "the customer" is not one person, it is a system of roles with
different powers and different motivations. Capture means understanding that system
and earning influence within it, legally and early, so that by the time the
requirement is written you are the obvious answer.

## Who actually decides

Assessment: map every one of these for each pursuit, with names, and keep it
current.

- **End user / warfighter / program user:** the people who will live with what you
  deliver. They define the real need and can be your strongest advocates.
- **Requirements owner / program manager (PM):** translates the need into a
  program and a requirement. Heavily shapes what the RFP asks for.
- **Program Executive Office (PEO) leadership:** the layer above the PM, with
  portfolio priorities and political awareness.
- **Contracting officer (KO):** the only person who can legally bind the
  government, and the gatekeeper of the acquisition process. Treat the KO with
  precision and respect; they enforce the rules.
- **Contracting officer's representative (COR):** the KO's technical eyes on
  performance once a contract is running. Vital for recompetes.
- **Source-selection authority (SSA) and evaluators:** who scores and who decides
  the award. You rarely engage them directly during a live procurement, but you
  design your proposal for them.
- **Budget / comptroller and testers (e.g. operational test):** the people who
  fund it and who must certify it works.

Assessment: the most common capture mistake is talking only to the people who like
you (often the end users) and never reaching the requirements owner and the
contracting shop who actually control the buy.

## Building the relationship: the call plan

A call plan is a deliberate schedule of who on your side meets whom on the
customer side, with a specific objective and a specific question for each contact.
Assessment: relationships are built before the procurement, because once a
solicitation is active the customer goes quiet and contact is tightly restricted.
The aim of each call is two-way: learn their priorities, constraints, and view of
the incumbent, and demonstrate (not assert) your credibility. Track what you
learned and what you will do next after every contact. A template is in
[chapter 10](10-templates-checklists.md).

## Shaping the requirement (legally)

Shaping is influencing the customer's understanding of the problem and the
solution space before the requirement is locked, so that the eventual RFP plays to
your strengths. FACT: this is expressly contemplated by the rules. FAR 15.201
provides for exchanges with industry before receipt of proposals, precisely to
improve the government's understanding of the market (acquisition.gov/far/part-15).
The legitimate shaping tools:

- **Sources-sought notices and Requests for Information (RFIs):** answer them, and
  answer them well. Your response can introduce capabilities, approaches, and even
  language the government may adopt into the requirement. This is one of the
  highest-leverage, lowest-cost shaping actions available.
- **Industry days and one-on-one sessions:** show up, ask sharp questions, and
  follow up. Use the one-on-ones to surface your discriminators as solutions to
  their stated problems.
- **White papers and capability briefings:** offer a concise, problem-focused
  paper that frames the mission gap and a path to close it. Done well, it educates
  the customer and seeds evaluation criteria that favor you.
- **Demonstrations, pilots, and prototypes (including OT prototypes and SBIRs):**
  the strongest shaping of all is letting the customer experience your capability
  and reduce their perceived risk firsthand. See
  [chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md) on OTAs and SBIR Phase III as on-ramps.
- **RFP draft comments:** when the government releases a draft RFP, submit
  thoughtful comments. Reasonable, mission-oriented suggestions often get adopted.

## The line you do not cross

Assessment, and it matters: shaping is education and advocacy, not collusion.
Never seek or accept non-public procurement-sensitive information, never help write
the actual solicitation in a way that rigs it (that creates an organizational
conflict of interest, see [chapter 8](08-relationships-ethics-oci.md)), and never
offer anything of value to influence an official. The Procurement Integrity Act
and the OCI rules are bright lines. Cross them and you lose the deal, the
relationship, and possibly far more. Good shaping is entirely compatible with
scrupulous integrity; in fact integrity is part of how you lower the customer's
perceived risk of choosing you.

## What good shaping looks like

Assessment: you know shaping worked when the released RFP describes the problem the
way you taught the customer to see it, weights the evaluation toward what you do
best, and feels, to your competitors, like it was written with you in mind, all
without a single improper act. That outcome is built over many honest
conversations and demonstrated competence, not bought.
