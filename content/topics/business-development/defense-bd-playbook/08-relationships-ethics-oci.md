# Chapter 8 — Relationships, ethics, and OCI

In defense BD, integrity is not a constraint on winning, it is part of how you win,
because the customer is choosing the contractor least likely to embarrass or fail
them. This chapter covers how to build relationships that compound, and the bright
lines that, if crossed, end the deal and sometimes the company.

## Relationships are the long game

Assessment: the customers and partners in your niche form a small, durable
community with long memories. Reputation is your most valuable BD asset, and it is
built over years of doing what you said you would do. Be useful before you are
needed: share genuine insight, make honest introductions, deliver flawlessly on the
contracts you already hold (today's performance is tomorrow's past-performance
score and reference). Assessment: the best BD is indistinguishable from being a
trustworthy, competent partner over time.

## The bright lines

These are not soft guidance. Treat them as absolute.

- **Procurement Integrity Act:** do not seek, obtain, or accept non-public
  source-selection information or another offeror's proprietary or bid information,
  and do not discuss employment with government officials who are personally and
  substantially involved in a procurement you are pursuing. Assessment: if someone
  offers you the competitor's price or the government's evaluation notes, that is a
  catastrophe to refuse and report, not an advantage.
- **Gifts and gratuities:** federal employees face strict limits on what they may
  accept. Assessment: when in doubt, offer nothing of value, no meals, no tickets,
  no favors. The cost of a violation dwarfs any benefit.
- **Bribery and improper influence:** never offer anything of value to influence an
  official act. This is criminal, full stop.
- **Truthfulness in proposals:** everything you certify and claim must be true. A
  knowing misrepresentation can trigger False Claims Act liability and suspension or
  debarment.
- **Lobbying and contact rules:** once a solicitation is active, honor the
  designated points of contact and the communication blackout. Going around the
  contracting officer damages trust and can taint the procurement.

## Organizational conflicts of interest (OCI)

An OCI exists when your other work could give you an unfair competitive advantage
or impair your objectivity. FACT: the FAR addresses OCI and requires agencies to
identify and resolve them (FAR Subpart 9.5). The classic categories:

- **Unequal access to information:** you held non-public information (from other
  work) that helps you compete. Resolved with firewalls and disclosure.
- **Biased ground rules:** you helped write the requirement or specifications, so
  you cannot then bid them. This is why helping "shape" must never cross into
  drafting the actual solicitation. Assessment: a biased-ground-rules OCI can
  disqualify you from the very work you helped define, the most expensive
  shaping mistake there is.
- **Impaired objectivity:** your work would require you to evaluate your own or a
  competitor's products. Resolved by structural separation or by not bidding.

Assessment: screen every pursuit for OCI early, because the cure (firewalls,
divestiture, recusal, or no-bid) can change your whole strategy, and an unmanaged
OCI hands a competitor easy grounds to protest you out.

## The mindset

Assessment: the line between aggressive, legitimate capture and a violation is
usually clear if you ask one question: am I trying to win by being the best and
best-understood option, or by getting something I am not entitled to? The first is
the whole point of this playbook. The second loses the deal, the relationship, and
potentially your eligibility to contract at all. When unsure, ask your contracts
and legal team before you act, not after.
