# Chapter 2 — Qualification, PWin, and gate reviews

The discipline that separates good BD organizations from busy ones is the
willingness to say no early and often. Assessment: bid-and-proposal (B&P) money is
finite, and every dollar spent chasing a low-probability deal is stolen from one
you could have won. Qualification exists to kill bad pursuits before they drain
you, and to concentrate force on the few you should win.

## Probability of win (PWin)

PWin is your honest estimate of the chance you take this award, expressed as a
percentage and re-scored at every gate. Assessment: its value is not the number
itself but the conversation it forces. A PWin built on hope is the most common
self-inflicted wound in BD.

Score PWin against concrete factors, for example:

- **Customer relationship:** do they know us, trust us, and prefer us?
- **Solution fit:** does the requirement match what we genuinely do best?
- **Past performance:** do we have directly relevant, well-rated work?
- **Incumbency:** are we the incumbent, or displacing one (incumbents win most
  recompetes, so displacing is hard)?
- **Shaping:** did we influence the requirement, or did a competitor?
- **Price posture:** can we be competitive at a price we can perform on?
- **Competition:** how many credible bidders, and how strong?

Be brutally honest, especially about shaping and relationship. Assessment: if you
cannot name the government people who want you to win and why, your real PWin is
low regardless of how good your solution is.

## A qualification checklist (adapt MEDDPICC for govcon)

The commercial MEDDPICC framework maps usefully onto defense capture. Use it as a
"do we actually understand this deal" test:

- **Metrics:** what outcome or mission gap does the customer need solved, and how
  will they measure success?
- **Economic buyer / decision authority:** who is the source-selection authority,
  and who shapes the requirement and the budget?
- **Decision criteria:** what will Section M say matters, technically and on
  price? (Best value tradeoff vs. lowest-price-technically-acceptable changes
  everything, see [chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md).)
- **Decision process:** what is the acquisition strategy and timeline, what
  vehicle, what set-aside?
- **Paper process:** the contractual and compliance path (vehicle eligibility,
  certifications, OCI).
- **Identify pain:** the mission or political pain driving the buy.
- **Champion:** who inside the customer wants you specifically to win?
- **Competition:** who else, and why might the customer pick them?

If you have blanks in this list, the job is to fill them, not to start writing.

## Gate reviews

A gate review is a scheduled, senior-leadership decision point where a pursuit
must earn the right to keep spending money. Assessment: gates only work if leaders
are genuinely willing to say no, and if no-bid is treated as a smart outcome, not
a failure. Common gates:

- **Gate 1 — Pursue?** (during qualification) Is this on strategy and worth
  capture resources? Approve a capture manager and a capture budget, or drop it.
- **Gate 2 — Prepare to bid?** (mid-capture) Has shaping moved PWin? Is the
  teaming and solution coming together? Commit B&P, or stop.
- **Gate 3 — Bid?** (at draft/final RFP) Final bid/no-bid. Are we compliant-able,
  competitive on price, and above our PWin threshold? Commit the full proposal
  investment.
- **Gate 4 — Submit?** (pre-submission) Is the proposal compliant, reviewed, and
  the price approved? Authorize submission.

## The bid/no-bid decision

Set a PWin floor for committing serious B&P (many organizations use something like
30 to 40 percent as a rough bar; pick yours deliberately). Assessment: walking
away from a low-PWin pursuit is a win, because it frees resources for a deal you
can take. The hardest and most valuable no-bids are the exciting opportunities
where you have no relationship and no shaping; discipline there is what protects
your win rate.

A no-bid is not wasted. Capture intelligence you gathered feeds the next
opportunity with that customer, and a polite, well-reasoned no-bid (sometimes
communicated as a decision to support a teammate instead) can preserve the
relationship.
