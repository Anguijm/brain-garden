# Chapter 14 — Further reading

A curated reading list for defense BD, organized by source and mapped to the
playbook chapter each item reinforces. Assessment: recommendations are based on
titles, public summaries, and publisher reputation, not full texts read in every
case, so treat the list as a shortlist worth your time rather than a verified
synthesis. For ongoing monitoring (feeds, alerts, where to watch week to week) see
[chapter 13 (staying current)](13-staying-current.md); this chapter is the deeper
readings to learn from. When a source should change the playbook itself, paste the
passage and I will synthesize it in, with honest labels per the vault's rules.

## Practitioner craft (start here)
The most directly actionable, because they are the frameworks this playbook is
built on.

- Shipley Associates, [Business Development Lifecycle Guide, Capture Guide, and
  Proposal Guide](https://www.shipleywins.com/products) (Larry Newman). The
  canonical practitioner references: a 7-phase, 96-step BD lifecycle, plus
  dedicated capture and proposal guides. The Proposal Guide is the basis for APMP
  certification. Paid books. These underpin
  [chapters 1](01-capture-lifecycle.md), [6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md), and
  [7](07-proposal-management.md).
- APMP, [Body of Knowledge and certification program](https://www.apmp.org/certification/)
  (Association of Proposal Management Professionals). Vendor-neutral best practice
  for bid, proposal, and capture work; the Foundation study glossary is free, the
  full BoK and certs need membership. Reinforces
  [chapters 2](02-qualification-pwin-gates.md) and
  [7](07-proposal-management.md).

## HBR articles
You have HBR access, so this lane is worth mining. Most links go to subscription
articles on hbr.org; a few are HBR Store products (case studies or reprints sold
separately), flagged "[HBR Store]".

### Decision quality, PWin, and avoiding overconfidence
For [chapter 2 (qualification, PWin, gates)](02-qualification-pwin-gates.md) and
[chapter 10 (Gen AI)](10-gen-ai-in-bd.md). This is the strongest cluster for BD,
because PWin, bid/no-bid, and price-to-win are all forecasts under uncertainty.

- Kahneman, Lovallo & Sibony, [The Big Idea: Before You Make That Big Decision…](https://hbr.org/2011/06/the-big-idea-before-you-make-that-big-decision)
  (2011). A 12-question checklist to catch bias in a team's recommendation. Read it
  as a template for tightening your gate reviews and bid/no-bid decisions.
- Lovallo & Kahneman, [Delusions of Success: How Optimism Undermines Executives' Decisions](https://hbr.org/2003/07/delusions-of-success-how-optimism-undermines-executives-decisions)
  (2003). Introduces the "outside view" and reference-class forecasting: estimate
  PWin from the actual win rate of your class of similar past pursuits, not an
  inside-view gut feel. Also names "competitor neglect," which is the bias a Black
  Hat ([chapter 6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md)) exists to fix.
- Kahneman, Sibony & Sunstein, [Noise](https://hbr.org/2016/10/noise) (2016).
  "Decision hygiene" against inconsistent judgment. Useful for making PWin and
  bid/no-bid scoring repeatable across capture managers.
- [Why Smart People (Sometimes) Make Bad Decisions](https://hbr.org/podcast/2021/05/why-smart-people-sometimes-make-bad-decisions)
  (2021, podcast). An accessible companion to the above.

### Win strategy and differentiation
For [chapter 6 (win strategy and competitive analysis)](06-win-strategy-competitive.md).

- [Discovering New Points of Differentiation](https://hbr.org/1997/07/discovering-new-points-of-differentiation)
  (1997). Differentiate at every customer touchpoint, not just the product. Feeds
  the discriminator hunt.
- [You Can Win Without Differentiation](https://hbr.org/2013/10/you-can-win-without-differentiation)
  (2013). A contrarian read, useful for commodity-like or LPTA buys.
- [The Secret to Becoming a Market Leader](https://hbr.org/2020/12/the-secret-to-becoming-a-market-leader)
  (2020). Target the customers you want rather than being customer-led, which is
  the portfolio logic of [chapter 1, phase 0](01-capture-lifecycle.md).

### Persuasion, storytelling, proposals, and orals
For [chapter 7 (proposal management)](07-proposal-management.md), and win themes in
[chapter 6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md).

- [The Art of Persuasion Hasn't Changed in 2,000 Years](https://hbr.org/2019/07/the-art-of-persuasion-hasnt-changed-in-2000-years)
  (2019). Ethos, pathos, logos: a clean structure for win themes.
- [A Great Sales Pitch Hinges on the Right Story](https://hbr.org/2024/05/a-great-sales-pitch-hinges-on-the-right-story)
  (2024) and [Using Stories to Persuade](https://hbr.org/2011/03/using-stories-as-a-tool-of-per)
  (2011). Frame the customer's mission gap as the story, with your solution as the
  resolution.
- [How to Give a Killer Presentation](https://hbr.org/2013/06/how-to-give-a-killer-presentation)
  (2013). For oral presentations.
- [6 Pitching Techniques to Use When Budgets Are Tight](https://hbr.org/2023/05/6-pitching-techniques-to-use-when-budgets-are-tight)
  (2023). Apt for cost-constrained and LPTA bids.

### Negotiation (teaming and price-to-win)
For [chapter 5 (teaming)](05-teaming-partnerships.md) and price-to-win in
[chapter 6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md).

- [Deal Making 2.0: A Guide to Complex Negotiations](https://hbr.org/2012/11/deal-making-20-a-guide-to-complex-negotiations)
  (Lax & Sebenius, 2012). Multi-front "negotiation campaigns" across many parties,
  which maps onto the diffuse defense buyer and multi-party teaming. Its six-step
  campaign framework is now synthesized into
  [chapter 4](04-customer-stakeholders-shaping.md) ("Run capture as a negotiation
  campaign").
- [What's Your Negotiation Strategy?](https://hbr.org/2020/07/whats-your-negotiation-strategy)
  (2020).
- [Negotiating with a Customer You Can't Afford to Lose](https://hbr.org/1988/11/negotiating-with-a-customer-you-cant-afford-to-lose)
  (1988). A classic, apt for recompetes and incumbency.
- [How Sales Teams Undercut Themselves with Longtime Clients](https://hbr.org/2026/05/how-sales-teams-undercut-themselves-with-longtime-clients)
  (2026). Premature concessions with incumbents, sharp for recompete pricing.

### Teaming and alliances
For [chapter 5 (teaming and partnerships)](05-teaming-partnerships.md).

- [Simple Rules for Making Alliances Work](https://hbr.org/2007/11/simple-rules-for-making-alliances-work)
  (2007).
- [Is Your Strategic Alliance Really a Sale?](https://hbr.org/1995/01/is-your-strategic-alliance-really-a-sale)
  (1995). A useful lens on prime/sub power dynamics.
- [Research: Joint Ventures that Keep Evolving Perform Better](https://hbr.org/2021/04/research-joint-ventures-that-keep-evolving-perform-better)
  (2021).
- *Strategic Alliances That Work: Selecting the Right Partner* and *Strategic
  Alliances: Three Ways to Make Them Work* [HBR Store] — partner-selection case
  material if you want depth.

### Pricing and price-to-win
For price-to-win in [chapter 6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md).

- [A Quick Guide to Value-Based Pricing](https://hbr.org/2016/08/a-quick-guide-to-value-based-pricing)
  (2016).
- [How Do You Know When the Price Is Right?](https://hbr.org/1995/09/how-do-you-know-when-the-price-is-right)
  (1995). A classic on pricing judgment.

### Customer relationships and stakeholder management
For [chapter 4 (customer, stakeholders, shaping)](04-customer-stakeholders-shaping.md)
and [chapter 8 (relationships)](08-relationships-ethics-oci.md).

- [How to Succeed at Key Account Management](https://hbr.org/2012/07/how-to-succeed-at-key-account)
  (2012). Team-based account relationships, mirroring the stakeholder map and call
  plan.
- [Build Customer Relationships That Last](https://hbr.org/1985/11/build-customer-relationships-that-last)
  (1985). A classic on relationship-building as the long game.
- *ABB and Caterpillar (A): Key Account Management* and *Siemens AG: Key Account
  Management* [HBR Store] — case studies if you want worked examples.

## Consulting and industry analysis
Mostly free to read online. Good for market context, customer priorities, and the
direction of acquisition reform (useful for shaping,
[chapter 4](04-customer-stakeholders-shaping.md)).

- McKinsey, [Aerospace & Defense insights](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/aerospace-and-defense/our-insights),
  notably [International defense sales: Opportunities, but not for all](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/aerospace-and-defense/our-insights/international-defense-sales)
  and [Strengthening America's defense industry](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/public-sector/our-insights/strengthening-american-defense-industry).
- Deloitte, [2026 Aerospace and Defense Industry Outlook](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/aerospace-defense/aerospace-and-defense-industry-outlook.html).
- PwC, [Aerospace and defense industry trends](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/industries/industrial-products/library/aerospace-and-defense-trends.html)
  and the A&D deals/M&A outlook.
- Assessment: a recurring theme across all three is the shift from cost-plus to
  outcome-based contracting and the rise of defense-tech entrants, both of which
  bear on price-to-win ([chapter 6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md)) and the
  acquisition landscape ([chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md)).

## Government and oversight (ground truth)
Free and authoritative. Cite these, not the commentary, when a fact has to be right.

- DAU, [Adaptive Acquisition Framework](https://aaf.dau.edu/aaf/aaf-pathways/)
  (Defense Acquisition University). The six acquisition pathways (urgent, middle
  tier, major capability, software, business systems, services); essential context
  for [chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md).
- GAO, [Bid Protest Annual Report to Congress](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-900695)
  and [Bid Protests: Key Features and Trends](https://files.gao.gov/reports/GAO-25-108652/index.html).
  The real numbers: a sustain rate around 16 percent and an effectiveness rate
  around 52 percent in recent years. Grounds the protest discussion in
  [chapter 3](03-acquisition-landscape.md).
- Also worth pulling directly: the DoD Source Selection Procedures guide, and CRS
  (Congressional Research Service) reports on defense acquisition and OTAs.

## Think tanks and market intelligence
- CSIS Defense-Industrial Initiatives Group, [DoD Contracts Report](https://www.csis.org/programs/defense-industrial-initiatives-group/dod-contracts-report),
  the annual [Defense Acquisition Trends](https://www.csis.org/analysis/defense-acquisition-trends-2023-preliminary-look),
  and [DoD Other Transaction Authority Trends](https://www.csis.org/analysis/department-defense-other-transaction-authority-trends-new-rd-funding-paradigm).
  The best public read on who DoD actually buys from, how concentrated it is among
  the Big Five, how much goes uncompeted, and how fast OTAs are growing. Directly
  feeds competitive analysis ([chapter 6](06-win-strategy-competitive.md)).
- RAND (acquisition and technology policy research) and Govini (the National
  Security Scorecard) are also worth watching for data-driven analysis. Govini is
  largely a paid platform.

## Books (the relationship and win side)
The playbook compresses these; the originals go deeper. All paid, widely available.

- Neil Rackham, *SPIN Selling*. The research-backed consultative-discovery method;
  pairs with the call plan in [chapter 4](04-customer-stakeholders-shaping.md).
- Matthew Dixon & Brent Adamson, *The Challenger Sale*. Teaching and reframing the
  customer's view; useful for shaping and win themes.
- Robert Miller & Stephen Heiman, *The New Strategic Selling*. Mapping the
  multi-stakeholder buying organization, the commercial analogue of the defense
  stakeholder map.
- Tom Sant, *Persuasive Business Proposals*. A proposal-writing classic for
  [chapter 7](07-proposal-management.md).
- Roger Fisher & William Ury, *Getting to Yes*, and David Lax & James Sebenius,
  *3-D Negotiation*. The negotiation foundations behind
  [chapter 5](05-teaming-partnerships.md) and the campaign framing in
  [chapter 4](04-customer-stakeholders-shaping.md). (3-D Negotiation is the book
  behind the "Deal Making 2.0" article listed above.)

## How to use this list
Assessment: read against a specific live pursuit, not in the abstract. Pick the
chapter you are working (say, building the capture plan), pull the two or three
items mapped to it, and mine them for one idea you can apply this week. If you read
only three things, make them the Shipley Capture Guide (the craft), the latest CSIS
DoD Contracts Report (the market), and the GAO Bid Protest Annual Report (the
risk). When something should change the playbook itself, paste it in and I will
synthesize and re-publish, with honest labels per the vault's rules.
