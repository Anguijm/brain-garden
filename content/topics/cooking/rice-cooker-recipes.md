# Rice cooker recipes: it does way more than rice

A rice cooker is basically a hands-off one-pot stove: dump things in, press a
button, walk away. This note has the few techniques that matter, then a handful
of actual meals you can make tonight, then a big "what else it can do" list.

A note on the recipes: the source recipe blogs hid their exact quantities behind
recipe-card widgets that didn't capture cleanly, so where I give measurements
below they're a **standard home version** of a well-known dish (labeled
Assessment), not a verbatim copy. The links go to the originals for precise
amounts. Everything here comes from cooking blogs, not a test kitchen, so treat
ratios as starting points and trust your own cooker.

## The four things worth knowing

1. **Water ratio (and why sources disagree).** You'll see 1:1, 1.5:1, and 2:1
   quoted. Assessment: the practical middle for plain **white rice is about 1 to
   1.25 cups water per cup of rinsed rice**, but this genuinely varies by cooker
   and rice type, so the real answer is *follow the fill line in your cooker's
   bowl or the manual*. Brown rice needs more water and longer (~30–45 min).
2. **Rinse the rice** until the water runs mostly clear. It rids excess surface
   starch so grains don't clump. The one exception some cooks make is sushi rice,
   where a little extra stickiness is wanted.
3. **Let it rest 5–15 minutes** after the cooker clicks to "warm," lid on. It
   finishes steaming and fluffs up. Then fork it, don't stir it to mush.
4. **Cooking a whole meal at once?** Rice goes on the **bottom**; slow ingredients
   (root veg) low, quick ones (thin meat, fish, leafy greens) on top. Reduce the
   rice water slightly because the other ingredients release moisture. To keep
   flavors separate, wrap something in a parchment parcel. Don't overfill, a
   blocked steam vent means uneven cooking.

A couple of safety notes: on simple on/off cookers, protein-heavy dishes
sometimes need you to **press "cook" a second time** after it clicks off. Use
wooden or silicone utensils (metal scratches the nonstick bowl). And **pressure**
rice cookers are a different animal, don't improvise sealed-lid recipes in one
without checking its manual.

## Five meals to actually make

### 1. One-tomato lazy rice (懒人番茄饭)
The viral one. A whole tomato steams in the center of the rice and melts into a
natural sauce when you mash it through.

Assessment (standard version): Rinse **2 rice-cooker cups of rice**, add water to
just under your normal line. Nestle **1 whole tomato** (core removed) in the
center. Scatter around it some sliced **Chinese sausage** (or bacon, smoked
sausage, or leftover rotisserie chicken), a drizzle of **soy sauce**, **oyster
sauce**, and a little **oil**. Cook on the normal rice setting. When done, mash
the soft tomato and fold everything together. Optional: a fried egg on top, or a
spoon of chili crisp. Vegetarian by skipping the meat.
Source for exact amounts: CookingBomb (S1).

### 2. Hotpot rice (火锅丸子饭)
Hotpot flavor, none of the setup. Rinsed rice + water, drop in a **hotpot
seasoning cube** (spicy, tomato, or mushroom base), then pile in frozen **fish
balls, shrimp balls, crab sticks** and any quick veg (napa, bok choy, mushrooms).
Cook normally; the cube flavors everything as it melts. A knob of butter stirred
in at the end makes it rich. (S1)

### 3. No-water soy-sauce chicken & veg (豉油鸡蔬菜锅)
A home-style braise where the **vegetables provide all the moisture** (called
无水料理, "no-water cooking"). Layer chicken (bone-in thighs/drumsticks are most
flavorful) marinated in soy + a little sugar/aromatics, with vegetables that
release water (cabbage, onion, mushrooms) underneath. **Add no water.** Cook; on
a cooler cooker you may need to press cook twice to finish the chicken. The pooled
sauce at the bottom is excellent tossed with noodles the next day. (S1)
Assessment: this technique relies on the veg, so don't skip them or scale the meat
way up, and make sure the chicken reaches a safe internal temperature (75°C/165°F).

### 4. Beef & zucchini rice (西葫芦牛肉焖饭)
A "deconstructed stir-fry." Thinly sliced beef tossed with a **cornstarch + soy
marinade** (the cornstarch keeps it silky), layered over rice and zucchini. The
rice soaks up the beef juices. Tip: freeze the beef 15–20 min before slicing thin.
Swap in chicken, pork, or firm tofu freely. (S1)

### 5. Banana chocolate cake
Yes, cake. Mash very ripe **bananas**, mix into a simple chocolate batter (flour,
cocoa, sugar, egg, oil/butter, a little baking powder, chocolate chips), grease
the bowl, pour in, and run the cook cycle, often twice, until set. For a lava
center, check around 40 minutes and pull it while the middle's still soft. A
teaspoon of instant coffee deepens the chocolate. (S1)

## What else the cooker can do

Assessment (across sources): treat it as a steamer, slow cooker, and porridge pot.

- **Breakfast:** oatmeal or steel-cut oats (oats + milk/water, porridge setting if
  you have one); congee/rice porridge; even pancake mix or hard-boiled eggs. Many
  cookers' **delay timer** lets you wake up to finished oatmeal.
- **Japanese rice classics:** takikomi gohan (rice cooked with dashi, soy, and
  mix-ins like mushroom or chicken), sekihan (red bean rice), oyakodon (chicken &
  egg over rice), omurice (ketchup rice).
- **Steamed veg:** use the steaming tray over the cooking rice, broccoli, carrots,
  green beans, or whole sweet potatoes.
- **Soups & stews:** Japanese curry, nikujaga (meat & potatoes), beef stew, butter
  chicken, tonjiru (pork miso soup).
- **Other grains:** quinoa cooks like rice at about **1:2 (quinoa:water)**; also
  polenta, risotto, beans.
- **Pasta, frittatas, even bread** are all doable in a pinch.

### Handy fixes
- **Lingering smell?** Cook 1 Tbsp baking soda in 3 cups water, then wash the bowl.
- **Overflowing?** Too much water or an unrinsed, starchy batch, cut the water a
  little and rinse next time.
- **Keep-warm** is safe for hours, but flavor/texture are best within a few hours.
